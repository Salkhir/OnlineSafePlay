Social platforms

TikTok, Instagram, Snapchat — platform-by-platform privacy settings, how algorithms target young users, age verification reality, and image sharing pressure.

High risk 10 min read Updated June 2026
The risks

What actually puts children at risk

🎯

Algorithms designed to maximise engagement

TikTok's For You Page and Instagram's Explore feed are optimised for time-on-app, not child wellbeing. Research shows they actively serve increasingly extreme content to keep users engaged — including self-harm content, eating disorder material, and radicalising content.

🔞

Age verification is largely ineffective

Most platforms require users to enter a date of birth. That is the entire age check. Children routinely lie about their age. The Online Safety Act 2023 requires platforms to implement age assurance — but enforcement is ongoing and loopholes remain widespread.

📷

Image pressure and self-image

Instagram and TikTok are built around physical appearance. Research consistently links heavy use in girls aged 11–17 to body dissatisfaction, lowered self-esteem, and increased rates of anxiety and depression. The platforms are aware of this from their own internal research.

📩

Direct messaging from strangers

Instagram, Snapchat and TikTok all allow unsolicited DMs in various forms. Adults posing as peers use these to make initial contact. Snapchat's disappearing messages feature is specifically exploited because children believe no record remains.

🌍

Public profiles visible to anyone

Most children set their accounts to public either deliberately (to get followers) or without understanding the default settings. A public profile means photos, location tags, school uniform in photos, and daily routines are visible to everyone on earth.

⏰

Permanence — what goes online stays online

Content shared on social media — even in Stories that "disappear" — can be screenshot, saved and shared by anyone who sees it. Children consistently underestimate this. Snapchat in particular creates a false sense of security around permanence.

⚠️ The age verification problem

Every major platform says it's for 13+. None of them effectively verify this. A study by Ofcom found that 45% of 8–12 year olds in the UK have a social media account, with most having lied about their age to create it.

The Online Safety Act 2023 places a legal duty on platforms to implement age assurance for content harmful to children — but "age assurance" does not mean verified identity. It can mean self-declaration. Enforcement by Ofcom is ongoing but many platforms are still non-compliant as of 2026.

The practical reality for parents: you cannot rely on platforms to stop your under-13 child from accessing social media. Parental controls, device-level restrictions, and conversations are the only effective barriers right now.

Settings that matter

Key privacy settings by platform

These are the highest-impact settings for each platform. Full step-by-step guides with screenshots are in our platform settings hub.

🎵 TikTok — critical settings

1Set account to Private (Profile → Privacy → Private account)
2Turn off Suggest account to others
3Restrict DMs to Friends only or off entirely
4Enable Family Pairing — links your account to your child's
5Set a daily screen time limit via Family Pairing (60–90 min recommended)
6Turn off Personalised ads based on activity

📸 Instagram — critical settings

1Set to Private account (Settings → Account Privacy)
2Turn off Show activity status
3Restrict DMs: Message controls → Only people you follow
4Set up Family Centre — parental supervision tools
5Enable Sensitive content control → Limit
6Turn off location in photos (device camera settings)

👻 Snapchat — critical settings

1Set Who can contact me to My Friends only
2Set Who can view my Story to My Friends
3Disable Snap Map or set to Ghost Mode — location invisible
4Turn off Quick Add (prevents strangers finding the account)
5Remind your child: screenshots still happen. Disappearing ≠ gone.

▶️ YouTube — critical settings

1Use YouTube Kids app for under-12s — not standard YouTube
2Enable Restricted Mode (filters adult content)
3Disable comments visibility on child's channel if they have one
4Set up Google Family Link — parental controls for Google accounts
5Regularly check Watch History — more revealing than you might expect
Action

Beyond the settings

Follow your child's accounts. Not to spy — to be visible. Children behave differently when they know a parent can see their content. Ask them to show you their feed rather than checking secretly.

Set phone-free times that include social media. Mealtimes, one hour before bed, and the first 30 minutes of the morning are the highest-impact times to restrict. Sleep quality improves measurably within days.

Watch for post-social-media mood shifts. A child who is consistently more anxious, withdrawn, or low in self-esteem after using social media is showing a pattern worth addressing directly — with the platform, with screen time, and with conversation.

Talk about what they share — not just what they see. Most parent focus is on what children consume online. Equally important is what they post: photos, location tags, and personal information shared publicly can be accessed by anyone.
