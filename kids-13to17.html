Real talk

If someone has images of you and is threatening to share them

This is a crime. Not your fault. And there are people who can stop it.

It doesn't matter how the images were taken, or what you were doing, or how well you thought you knew the person. The person threatening you has committed a criminal offence under the Online Safety Act 2023.

The shame is not yours to carry. The police, CEOP, and Childline will not judge you. They have seen this before — many times. They exist to stop the person doing this to you.

Do not pay them. It never ends with one payment — it escalates immediately. Every single time. Report to CEOP at ceop.police.uk or call Childline on 0800 1111. Both are confidential.

Set the record straight

Myth vs fact

Myth

"If I sent the image, it's my fault"

Sending something to one trusted person is not consent for them to share it with anyone else. Sharing an intimate image without permission is a criminal offence in the UK.

Fact

Sharing without consent is illegal — full stop

Under the Online Safety Act 2023, sharing intimate images without consent carries up to 2 years in prison. AI-generated deepfake images of real people are also now included.

Myth

"An AI friend is safer than a real person"

AI companions are designed to keep you engaged — not to act in your best interest. The company profits from your continued use. That's not friendship.

Fact

AI chatbots do not have feelings or loyalty

They generate responses based on patterns. They don't miss you when you're gone. Relying on AI for emotional support can make real relationships harder, not easier.

Things worth knowing

The legal reality

Sexting between under-18s

Technically illegal in the UK even between consenting teens. Police can use discretion, but images can end up on harmful sharing sites regardless of the original intent.

AI deepfake images

Creating or sharing AI-generated intimate images of real people without consent is a criminal offence in England and Wales as of 2024 — regardless of age.

Grooming & exploitation

Adults who manipulate young people online for sexual purposes face serious prison sentences. No adult can legally claim they "didn't know" you were under 18.

For when it's not you

Helping a friend — when you're worried about someone else

Listen without judging

Let them talk. Don't minimise it ("it's probably fine") or catastrophise. Just be there. "I'm really glad you told me" goes a long way — further than you might think.

Encourage them to tell a trusted adult

You don't have to carry this alone, and neither do they. Help them think of who they trust — a parent, a teacher, a school counsellor. Offer to go with them if that would help.

If they're in danger, tell an adult yourself

A real friend sometimes has to break a secret to keep someone safe. That's not a betrayal — it's the most loyal thing you can do. Their safety matters more than the secret.
