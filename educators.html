Current risks relevant to schools right now
Emerging

AI deepfakes in school settings

AI nudification tools being used to create images of classmates — already reported in UK schools. Existing law applies.

Rising

AI companion dependency

Children forming emotional bonds with AI personas. Relevant to pastoral care, PSHE and safeguarding referral thinking.

Ongoing

Sextortion — increasing in secondary age

Organised criminal groups targeting teenagers. Often surfaces in school when a student discloses. See our safeguarding contacts below.

Ideas for the classroom

Topics worth exploring

These are suggestions for the kinds of things that tend to work well in a session — not a prescribed curriculum. Every school is different, and the right approach will depend on your students and context.

Privacy ~45 min

What's private? Keeping our details safe

A session helping young children understand what information is private and who they can share it with online. Works well as a circle-time discussion or with simple sorting activities — private/not private. Connects naturally to the PANTS rule most children already know from PSHE.

Discussion · sorting activity See what children learn →
Strangers online ~45 min

Who could be online? Trusted adults and strangers

Exploring the difference between trusted adults and strangers in an online context. Role play and discussion tend to work well here — children can act out what they'd do and who they'd tell. Connects to existing safeguarding language children already have.

Role play · discussion See what children learn →
Gaming ~60 min

Who are you really talking to in games?

Exploring how online identity works and why people aren't always who they say they are. Works well with scenarios children can discuss in pairs or small groups. Key question: "What would you do if someone asked to move the chat somewhere else?" Children are often more knowledgeable about specific games than staff — leaning into that works well.

Scenarios · small group discussion See what children learn →
Bullying ~60 min

When someone is mean online — what it is and what to do

Helping children recognise cyberbullying, understand how it differs from a one-off falling out, and practise the steps they'd take. Including how to support a friend is often more powerful than focusing only on what to do if it happens to you — children respond well to the "helping a friend" framing.

Scenarios · class discussion See what children learn →
Privacy ~50 min

Screenshots are forever

Exploring the permanence of online content through relatable scenarios. The "screenshot test" — would I be OK if my teacher or parent saw this? — is a practical mental check children can take away. Works well as a paired discussion activity with increasingly complex scenarios.

Paired discussion · scenarios See what children learn →
AI ~75 min

AI companions — what's really going on?

A critical thinking session on how AI companion apps work and why they feel emotionally real. Teens respond well to being shown the business model — "the app makes money the more time you spend on it" — rather than being told it's dangerous. Myth-busting activities and open debate tend to generate good discussion. Our guide on AI chatbots and AI impersonation have background reading for staff preparing the session.

Myth-busting · debate · discussion See what teens learn →
Image safety ~90 min

Image sharing, consent & the law

One of the most important sessions for secondary students — covering what consent means for images and videos, UK law under the Online Safety Act 2023, and the reality of AI deepfakes. Framing this around what young people need to know to protect themselves — rather than telling them what not to do — tends to land better. Real statistics from the IWF and CEOP are powerful here. See our AI impersonation guide for current data.

Statistics · case studies · discussion See what teens learn →
Social media ~75 min

Social media, algorithms & how they affect you

Media literacy for the social media age — how recommendation algorithms work, why comparison culture is structurally built into platforms, and what the evidence shows about impact on mental health. Showing students how the algorithm works (rather than telling them it's bad for them) is generally more effective. Our top 10 social platforms guide has sourced data useful for staff preparation.

Media literacy · reflection · discussion See what teens learn →

When something happens

Incident response protocols

For DSLs and all teaching staff. These protocols are aligned with KCSIE 2024 and the Online Safety Act 2023.

A child has made a disclosure — do this now

Step 1

Listen and believe

Don't ask leading questions. Say "thank you for telling me." Do not promise confidentiality.

Step 2

Record exactly

Write the child's own words with time and date. Do not paraphrase or interpret.

Step 3

Refer to your DSL

Pass immediately to the Designated Safeguarding Lead. Do not investigate yourself.

Step 4

Reassure the child

Tell them they were right to tell you, it is not their fault, and trusted adults will help.

Whole-school thinking

Ideas for assemblies & whole-school moments

These are suggestions for how online safety might work beyond the classroom — not a fixed programme. Schools find very different approaches work for them.

A primary-focused assembly

Something that tends to work well: a short (15–20 min) session built around a simple scenario children can respond to together. "What would you do if...?" engages even the youngest children. A single clear take-home message — rather than a list of rules — is more likely to stick. Always worth ending by reminding children who they can talk to if something worries them.

15–20 min

A secondary-focused session

With older students, real data tends to land harder than scenarios. Statistics from the IWF, CEOP, or the Children's Commissioner on deepfakes, sextortion, or AI-generated images make the abstract feel concrete. Framing it as "things worth knowing" rather than "things you shouldn't do" is usually better received. Our AI impersonation guide and top 10 social platforms guide have current cited data that works well here.

20–30 min

Safer Internet Day — 7 February

A useful annual hook to bring online safety into form time, assemblies, and lessons across the school in a coordinated way. The UK Safer Internet Centre publishes free resources each year at saferinternet.org.uk. It also gives a natural hook for parent communications — families tend to engage more when they know it's a national moment rather than just the school raising concerns.

Staff development

Ideas for staff sessions

A few topics that tend to be worth covering with staff — whether in an INSET slot, a staff meeting, or as part of DSL-specific training. These are starting points, not a programme.

What does online harm actually look like today?

The platforms, language, and risk patterns change quickly. A session helping all staff understand what children are actually encountering right now — not two years ago — tends to shift awareness significantly. Our guides on gaming, social platforms, and AI have current, sourced information useful for whoever is leading the session.

INSET or staff meeting · all staff

DSL-specific: current legislation and referral pathways

Keeping across KCSIE updates, the Online Safety Act 2023, and what platforms are now legally required to do. Understanding which incidents require a CEOP referral vs a local authority route. Our AI impersonation guide covers the latest developments on deepfakes and UK law, which is moving quickly.

DSL-specific · extended session

Recognising the signs — what to look for

Behavioural indicators that something may be wrong online — and how to respond well if a child discloses something. Our warning signs guide covers the specific changes worth noticing. The most important thing to convey to all staff: how they respond in the first few minutes of a disclosure determines whether the child comes back.

Staff meeting · all staff

AI in the classroom — literacy and risks together

Helping staff understand how to talk about AI tools accurately — what they can and can't do, the companion AI risks, and how deepfake technology works — so they can handle questions from students with confidence. Our AI impersonation guide and AI chatbots guide have background reading for staff preparing this.

Teaching staff · 45–60 min

Policy thinking

What good policy might look like

Every school's context is different, and policy needs to reflect that. These are the areas a strong online safety policy tends to cover — along with links to existing frameworks that schools find useful as a starting point.

Online safety policy

A good online safety policy tends to cover: the school's approach to online safety education; how incidents are identified and responded to; roles and responsibilities (DSL, governors, staff, students, parents); how the policy connects to existing safeguarding, behaviour, and PSHE policies; and how it's reviewed and kept current.

The South West Grid for Learning (SWGfL) maintains a free, regularly updated online safety policy template that schools across England use as a starting point. It's aligned to current statutory requirements including KCSIE 2024 and can be adapted to your school's context.

Emerging area — worth thinking about now

A school AI use policy

AI use in schools is moving faster than most policy frameworks. A good starting point for an AI policy considers: which AI tools students may use (inside and outside school), what appropriate and inappropriate use looks like, how staff should address AI-generated work, how to talk about AI companion risks, and what to do if AI tools are being used harmfully (e.g. deepfake images of peers).

The Department for Education published AI guidance for schools in 2024, and Jisc maintains an AI in education policy template that schools and colleges are finding useful as a structural starting point. Neither is prescriptive — they're frameworks to adapt.

DfE AI guidance for schools → Jisc AI policy template →

Acceptable use — students and staff

Acceptable use agreements work best when students have been involved in shaping them — not just asked to sign something handed to them. A good student AUP is written in plain language, covers what the school's devices and network can and cannot be used for, makes expectations about social media and messaging clear, and explains what happens if the agreement is not followed. The SWGfL template above includes an AUP section. Internet Matters also has guidance on what pupil AUPs tend to include.

Internet Matters — AUP guidance →

Staff and social media

A useful area for policy to be clear on: the distinction between personal and professional accounts, expectations around connecting with students or parents on personal platforms, and what staff should consider before posting content that might be seen in a professional context. The NSPCC and NASUWT both have guidance documents schools use when putting together their own approach.

NSPCC safer working practice → NASUWT social media guidance →
