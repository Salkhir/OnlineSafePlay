Chat & messaging

Discord, WhatsApp, DMs — how grooming progresses online, what the law says about sexting, and how to talk to your child about it.

Medium-high risk 9 min read Updated April 2026
The risks

What makes messaging dangerous

🎮

Moving off-game to private apps

The most common grooming pattern starts in a game with unmoderated chat. After weeks of friendly contact, the suggestion comes to "move to Discord/WhatsApp so we can talk properly." This removes all platform moderation and parental visibility.

🖥️

Discord — significant risks

Discord is primarily known as a gaming platform but functions as a social network. Servers can be public or private. Adults recruit children into servers that appear innocent before exposing them to inappropriate content or contact. No effective age verification.

📱

WhatsApp group dynamics

School WhatsApp groups are common and largely unmoderated. Cyberbullying, screenshot sharing, and inappropriate content are frequently distributed through these groups. Once a message is in a group, it is impossible to retrieve.

💌

Sexting — peer pressure and misuse

Pressure to send intimate images exists in peer relationships as well as from adults. Children as young as 11 report receiving requests for images. Once sent, images can be shared without consent, used for blackmail, or published on harmful sites.

💰

Sextortion — financial and further image demands

Criminals pose as peers, build trust, obtain one intimate image, then demand money or more images under threat of sharing the original. This is now the fastest growing form of child sexual exploitation in the UK. It targets boys disproportionately.

🔒

End-to-end encryption — double-edged

WhatsApp and Signal use end-to-end encryption, which protects legitimate privacy but also makes it impossible for platforms to detect harmful content or for parents to monitor conversations through technical means.

Understanding grooming

How it typically progresses

Grooming is not sudden — it follows a pattern over weeks or months. Recognising the stages helps parents intervene before harm occurs.

1

Targeting

The perpetrator identifies a child who appears lonely, isolated, unhappy at home, or seeking attention online. Gaming platforms, social media comments sections, and Discord servers are common targeting environments.

2

Gaining trust

Weeks of friendly, supportive contact. The perpetrator is consistently kind, interested in the child's life, and often gifts in-game items or money. They make the child feel uniquely understood. This stage can last months.

3

Filling needs

The perpetrator positions themselves as the person who understands the child best — better than parents or friends. They may encourage the child to share problems at home, creating emotional dependency and distance from family.

4

Isolation

The child is encouraged to keep the relationship secret. They may be told parents wouldn't understand, or that friends would be jealous. The private communication channel — Discord, WhatsApp — is established at this stage.

5

Desensitisation

Conversations gradually shift toward sexual topics, introduced as normal or educational. The child is exposed to sexual content incrementally, making later requests feel less alarming than they would have initially.

6

Maintaining control

Once sexual contact or images are involved, threats, guilt, or emotional manipulation are used to maintain secrecy. The child may feel responsible or fear getting in trouble — which is why blame-free disclosure conversations are so important.

⚖️ The law — what parents and children need to know

Sexting between under-18s

Creating, possessing or sharing intimate images of anyone under 18 is illegal in the UK — even if both parties are under 18 and both consented. Police can use discretion but the law is clear.

Sharing without consent

Under the Online Safety Act 2023, sharing intimate images of any person without their consent is a criminal offence carrying up to 2 years in prison. AI-generated images are included.

Sextortion

Using intimate images to threaten or blackmail someone is a serious criminal offence. The National Crime Agency and CEOP deal with these cases. Do not pay — report immediately.

Action

What parents can do

Know which apps your child uses for messaging. Ask directly. Many children have secondary accounts or use lesser-known apps specifically because parents don't know about them. Discord, Telegram, and Kik are frequently used as "hidden" messaging platforms.

Restrict Discord to friends only. In Discord settings, disable the ability to receive DMs from people who share a server but aren't friends. This prevents strangers in gaming servers from contacting your child privately.

Teach your child the "move to private chat" warning sign. If someone they met online suggests moving to a different, more private app to talk, that is a red flag. They should tell you immediately — no matter how friendly the person seems.

Have the sexting conversation early — before it happens. Tell your child: "If anyone ever asks you to send a photo of yourself, the answer is always no — and you always tell me. You won't be in trouble. I just want to keep you safe." This conversation should happen by age 10.

If sextortion is happening — do not pay. Contact CEOP immediately, screenshot all messages before blocking, and call Childline on 0800 1111 for support. See our full sextortion protocol.
