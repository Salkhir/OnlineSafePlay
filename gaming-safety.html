Gaming safety

Who your child is really talking to, loot boxes, in-game spending, and how to set up controls on every console — a complete guide for parents.

High risk 8 min read Updated June 2026
The risks

What parents need to know

💬

In-game chat — the biggest risk

Most games include voice and text chat. Players cannot verify who they are talking to. Adults routinely misrepresent themselves as children. Group chats in games like Roblox and Fortnite are where the majority of grooming contact begins — not social media.

🎰

Loot boxes and gambling mechanics

Loot boxes — randomised reward crates bought with real money — are classified as gambling in Belgium and the Netherlands. UK law hasn't caught up. Children as young as 8 are spending significant sums. This can also normalise gambling behaviour for later life.

💸

In-game spending and V-Bucks

Games use virtual currencies to distance children from real money. A Fortnite skin costs "800 V-Bucks" — not "£6.49." Children with access to a card linked to a console account can rack up hundreds of pounds before a parent notices.

📺

Live streaming — Twitch and YouTube Live

Children who stream their gameplay are broadcasting their voice, sometimes their face, and often their location (via school logos, local landmarks). Chat on live streams is largely unmoderated and can include inappropriate contact.

🌙

Gaming addiction and sleep loss

Game design deliberately triggers reward loops that make stopping feel difficult. This is engineered, not a character flaw. Late-night gaming is one of the most consistent factors in poor school performance and mental health issues in children aged 8–16.

📲

Moving off-game to private chat

A common grooming pattern: make contact in a game, build trust over weeks, then suggest moving to Discord, WhatsApp or Snapchat "so we can talk properly." The private platform removes all moderation and parental visibility.

Action checklist

What to do right now

Set up Family Controls on your console. Xbox Family Safety, PlayStation Family Management, and Nintendo Family Group all allow you to restrict chat with strangers, set spending limits, and set play time limits. See our platform settings hub for step-by-step guides.

Remove saved payment methods from console accounts. Or set a PIN that must be entered for every purchase. Do this before you do anything else — it prevents surprise charges immediately.

Restrict or disable chat with strangers. On Roblox, enable Account Restrictions. On Fortnite, disable voice chat or restrict to friends only. On Xbox, set communication to "friends only" in Family Settings.

Set a daily play time limit. NHS guidance suggests no more than 2 hours of recreational screen time for school-age children. Console parental controls can enforce this automatically.

Establish a device curfew. All devices — including consoles — out of bedrooms by 9pm for under-12s, 10pm for teens. Blue light and stimulation from gaming directly disrupts sleep.

Know what games your child plays and with whom. Ask to see their friends list. Ask who the usernames belong to. Be curious, not suspicious — the conversation matters more than the surveillance.

💬 How to start the conversation

For ages 7–10

"I know you love playing [game name] — that's brilliant. Can you show me who your friends are on there? Are they people you know from school, or people you've met in the game?"

Keep it curious and conversational. Sit next to them while they play sometimes — normalise involvement.

For ages 11–14

"I've been reading about some stuff that happens in gaming and I want to make sure you know about it too — not to scare you, just so you know what to do if it happens. Has anyone you've met in a game ever asked you to move to a different chat?"

Frame it as sharing knowledge, not interrogation. Listen more than you talk.

For ages 14+

"I trust you and I'm not trying to spy on you. I just want to check in — is there anything that's happened online that's felt a bit off? No judgement, I just want to know you're OK."

Teens respond to being treated as capable. Avoid accusatory language.

Warning signs

Red flags in gaming behaviour

Switching screens or closing the game quickly when you enter the room

Especially if this is a new behaviour — sudden secrecy around the screen is a consistent early indicator.

Talking about a new "friend" they met in a game who you've never heard of

Especially if this person seems to be in frequent contact, or if your child becomes protective of the relationship.

Receiving gifts, game currency, or items they can't explain

Gifting in-game items is a common early grooming tactic — building obligation and affection before making a request.

Gaming until very late at night or waking early to play

Sleep disruption from gaming is very common and affects school performance, mood, and physical health significantly.
